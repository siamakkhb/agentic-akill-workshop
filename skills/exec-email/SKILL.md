## When to Use
- Keyword trigger: "exec email", "email to VP", "write to leadership", "draft for director", "executive update", "email to SVP", "C-suite email"
- Context trigger: PM needs to communicate a launch update, escalation, risk flag, decision request, roadmap summary, or incident post-mortem to L7+ Amazon leaders
- Workflow trigger: end of a planning cycle, pre-launch, post-incident, decision checkpoint, or any moment requiring senior leadership alignment or approval

## Quick Reference

### Draft a New Exec Email
Collect from the PM: audience (specific exec or group), purpose (one sentence), key points (3–5 bullets), and background input (notes, docs, data).
Structure the output as: Subject → TL;DR → Context → Ask/Decision → Next Steps.
Default tone is formal; assess context and propose an alternative if warranted.

### Adjust Tone for Audience
Ask who the recipient is and the nature of the relationship (known collaborator vs. formal stakeholder).
Formal is the default for L7+ Amazon leaders. Shift warmer only for celebrations or established informal relationships. Stay formal for escalations and bad news regardless.

### Sharpen an Existing Draft
Check: Is the subject specific and action-oriented? Is the TL;DR standalone in 3 sentences? Is there exactly one clear ask? Is every sentence earning its place?
Cut anything that doesn't add meaning. Push vague points to specifics — use data where available, flag honestly where it's not.

## Full Documentation

### Tone and Audience Calibration
Amazon L7+ leaders (Directors, VPs, SVPs) operate in high-signal, low-patience environments. Default to formal, direct, and fact-based.
Assess context before locking in tone:
- **Formal (default):** Decision requests, escalations, risk flags, incident summaries — e.g., "I am writing to flag a risk to our Q3 launch timeline..."
- **Warm but crisp:** Recognition, team win announcements, known collaborator — e.g., "Wanted to share a quick win the team shipped this week..."
- **Never:** Hedging language ("I think", "sort of", "maybe"), filler phrases ("Hope this finds you well"), or passive voice when action is needed.

### Email Structure — Detailed
Each section serves a specific purpose — do not merge or skip them:

**Subject line** — signals urgency and required action before the email is opened.
- Weak: "Product Update" | Strong: "Decision Needed: Feature X Launch — Q3 Miss Risk if Not Approved by Friday"

**TL;DR** — written as if it's the only section they read. Max 3 sentences: situation + ask or impact.
- Example: "Feature X is on track for Sept 15 launch. We need VP approval on the revised pricing model by Sept 5 to proceed. Delay beyond that date risks a 6-week slip."

**Context** — only what's needed to understand the situation. No history they already know. Use data where available; if unavailable, say so — never fabricate.

**Ask / Decision** — one primary ask, stated unambiguously. If multiple asks are unavoidable, rank them 1, 2, 3. Always include a deadline if one exists.

**Next Steps** — format as: [Owner] will [action] by [date]. Always include the PM's own next step.

### Error Handling
- **Audience not specified:** Do not draft. Ask who the recipient is — tone, framing, and level of detail depend entirely on this.
- **Purpose is vague:** Do not guess the intent of an exec communication. Ask for one clear sentence on why this email is being sent.
- **Key points are missing or too broad:** Draft based on background input but explicitly flag every assumption made — e.g., "I assumed the primary ask is approval of X; please confirm."
- **No background input provided:** Ask for at least one source (meeting notes, doc, data) before drafting. Do not fabricate context.
- **Email has multiple asks:** Flag this to the PM. Recommend splitting into separate emails or ranking asks explicitly. Exec emails with multiple unclear asks are frequently ignored.
